## Core Idea

**Summary**:

David Allen starts from a simple premise: your brain is for having ideas, not holding them. When commitments, tasks and worries float freely in your mind, they create stress and steal mental energy — even when you're not working on them. The GTD (Getting Things Done) method proposes getting everything out of your head and into a reliable external system, following five steps:

1. **Capture** — Record everything that gets your attention (tasks, ideas, commitments) in external inboxes (paper, app, email), without trying to organize them yet.
2. **Clarify (Process)** — For each captured item, ask: "does this require action?" If not, discard, file as reference, or save for "someday/maybe". If it does require action, decide what is the **next physical, visible action** needed. If it takes less than 2 minutes, do it now. If not, delegate or schedule it.
3. **Organize** — Put each item in the right place: next actions lists (organized by context like "on the phone" or "at the computer"), calendar (only time-specific commitments), projects list (any outcome requiring more than one action), and "waiting for" list (delegated items).
4. **Review** — Do a **Weekly Review** to update your lists, check ongoing projects and keep your system reliable. Without this review, the system loses trust and your mind goes back to hoarding worries.
5. **Engage (Do)** — When choosing what to do next, use four criteria: context (where you are and what you have available), time available, energy available, and priority.

Allen also introduces the concept of **"mind like water"** — a mental state of appropriate and proportional response to situations, only possible when there are no "open loops" taking up mental space. He also advocates for thinking in **altitude levels** (from daily tasks to 50,000-foot life goals) to align daily tasks with bigger purposes.

**Practical Example**:

Instead of thinking "I need to organize my kid's birthday party" (a vague project that creates anxiety), the GTD method asks you to define the next concrete action: "call the caterer X and ask for a quote". This clarity eliminates procrastination because your brain knows exactly what physical step to take next.

**Why it matters**:

GTD offers a practical, tested system for reducing mental overload and increasing clarity about what to do in each moment, especially valuable for people dealing with multiple projects and responsibilities at once — without relying on willpower or memory.

## Implementation Checklist

### Before Starting
- [ ] Choose the capture tools you'll use (notebook, notes app, email)
- [ ] Reserve a 2–4 hour block for an initial "mind sweep"
- [ ] Define where your lists will live: next actions, projects, waiting for, someday/maybe

### During Implementation
- [ ] Capture literally everything that's in your head in one single list (the "mind sweep")
- [ ] Process each item by asking "does this require action?" and defining the next physical action
- [ ] Apply the 2-minute rule: if it's fast, do it now
- [ ] Organize time-specific commitments only on your calendar — never mix them with task lists
- [ ] Schedule your first Weekly Review

### After Implementation
- [ ] Do the Weekly Review every week without fail
- [ ] Check if your context lists (e.g., "at the computer", "errands") still reflect your real routine
- [ ] Periodically evaluate if your projects are aligned with your life goals (long-term vision)

## Connections to Other Books

### Related Books
- **Essentialism** (Greg McKeown) — complements GTD by helping filter which projects actually deserve to enter the system.
- **The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People** (Stephen Covey) — provides the "why" and long-term vision that GTD uses as a backdrop (the "altitude levels").
- **Deep Work** (Cal Newport) — deepens the topic of attention and focus, complementing GTD's organization with execution focus.
- **Atomic Habits** (James Clear) — focuses on building lasting habits, useful for maintaining the Weekly Review discipline.

### Suggested Reading Order
1. The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People (purpose and vision)
2. Getting Things Done (system and organization)
3. Essentialism (prioritization and focus)
4. Deep Work (execution with deep concentration)

## Personal Evaluation

### Strengths
- Extremely practical and applicable system from the first chapter
- Reduces anxiety by emptying your mind of unresolved commitments
- Flexible enough to adapt to digital or analog tools

### Weaknesses
- Can feel bureaucratic or cumbersome to maintain at first
- Requires constant discipline (especially the Weekly Review), which many people abandon
- Some sections about office tools are dated (references to physical folders, faxes)

### For Whom
- Professionals with multiple simultaneous projects and responsibilities
- People who feel mental overload or struggle with prioritization
- Those who want a structured system instead of relying on willpower

### Not For
- Those looking for motivation or life purpose (this book is about method, not about "why")
- People who prefer simpler, more minimalist systems without multiple lists and categories

## Is It Worth Reading the Full Book?

If you want to dive deeper into the nuances of each step (especially "clarify" and "organize"), detailed workflow examples, and variations of the system for different types of professions — rich content this summary doesn't cover in detail.

## Summarizer Notes

GTD is one of the few productivity books that has stood the test of time, being a reference even decades after release, including influencing most modern task management apps (Todoist, Things, Notion, etc.). The biggest challenge isn't understanding the method — it's actually simple — but maintaining the Weekly Review discipline, which is what guarantees the system's long-term reliability.

## Quick Recap (30 Seconds)

**Remember the core in 30 seconds**:

Get everything out of your head and into a reliable external system: capture everything, clarify what requires action, organize into context lists, review weekly, and execute based on context, time, energy and priority.

**Immediate Action**:

Do a "mind sweep" right now: grab paper and pen (or a notes app) and write, unfiltered, everything that's taking up mental space — tasks, worries, ideas, commitments. Then pick the 3 most urgent items and define the next physical action for each.
